The bias–variance decomposition is the first place most students meet the idea that *test error has structure*. This explainer is a stand-alone refresher: what the decomposition says, when it holds exactly, and where it stops being the right lens. It belongs to [[machine-learning-theory]] and sits alongside [[research-papers|other paper explainers]].

<Aside>
Margin notes appear inline on mobile and as a quiet annotation column on desktop. They are useful for technical asides without breaking the main argument.
</Aside>

<Callout type="tip" title="TL;DR">
For squared loss, expected test error splits cleanly into three independent pieces: how wrong the *average* learner is (**bias²**), how much the learner *jitters* across training sets (**variance**), and the irreducible noise floor σ². Picking a model class is choosing how to spend a budget across these three.
</Callout>

## Setup

We fix a data-generating distribution $\mathcal{D}$ over $(X, Y) \in \mathcal{X} \times \mathbb{R}$, with conditional mean $f^\star(x) = \mathbb{E}[Y \mid X = x]$ and noise variance $\sigma^2(x) = \mathrm{Var}(Y \mid X = x)$. A *learner* is a function $\hat f$ produced from a training set $S \sim \mathcal{D}^n$; the randomness in $\hat f$ comes from $S$.

<Theorem label="Definition" title="point-wise risk" id="def:risk">
The point-wise expected squared loss at a query $x_0$ is
$$
R(x_0) \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{S,\, Y_0}\Big[\,(\,\hat f_S(x_0) - Y_0\,)^2 \,\Big],
$$
where $Y_0$ is a fresh draw from $\mathcal{D}(\,\cdot \mid X = x_0)$.
</Theorem>

<MathBlock title="The decomposition we will prove">
With $\bar f(x_0) = \mathbb{E}_S[\hat f_S(x_0)]$,
$$
R(x_0) \;=\; \underbrace{(\bar f(x_0) - f^\star(x_0))^2}_{\text{bias}^2} \;+\; \underbrace{\mathbb{E}_S\!\big[(\hat f_S(x_0) - \bar f(x_0))^2\big]}_{\text{variance}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sigma^2(x_0)}_{\text{noise}}.
$$
</MathBlock>

The three terms are *additive* and *non-negative*. The first depends only on the learner-on-average; the second only on its jitter; the third on the world.

## The decomposition

<Theorem label="Theorem 1" title="Bias–variance decomposition" id="thm:bv" accent="accent">
For any learning rule whose output $\hat f_S$ has finite second moment at $x_0$,
$$
R(x_0) \;=\; \big(\bar f(x_0) - f^\star(x_0)\big)^2 \;+\; \mathrm{Var}_S\!\big[\hat f_S(x_0)\big] \;+\; \sigma^2(x_0).
$$
</Theorem>

<Proof>
Add and subtract $\bar f(x_0)$ and $f^\star(x_0)$ inside the square, then expand. The three cross terms vanish: $\mathbb{E}_S[\hat f_S(x_0) - \bar f(x_0)] = 0$ by definition of $\bar f$, and $Y_0 - f^\star(x_0)$ has mean zero and is independent of $\hat f_S$ (different randomness). What remains are the three squared terms.
</Proof>

<Aside>
The independence step is the one most often skipped. It uses that $Y_0$ is a *fresh* draw — not one of the training labels — so it is independent of $\hat f_S$.
</Aside>

## Visualising the trade-off

The classical picture is the U-shape of expected test error as model complexity grows: $\text{bias}^2$ falls, variance rises, and their sum has a unique minimum.

<TwoColumns>
<div>

### What pushes bias down

Adding capacity to the model class — more features, deeper trees, a larger network — lets $\bar f$ track $f^\star$ more faithfully, especially in regions with curvature.

</div>
<div>

### What pushes variance up

The same added capacity lets the learner over-fit individual training sets. Two independent draws of $S$ disagree more, so $\mathrm{Var}_S[\hat f_S]$ grows.

</div>

## Where the lens breaks down

<Callout type="warning" title="Squared loss only">
The clean three-term split is a property of *squared loss*. For 0-1 loss the analogue exists but is not a sum of independent pieces; for log-loss it requires care with the link function.
</Callout>

<Callout type="info" title="Effective complexity">
What pushes variance up is *effective* complexity, not parameter count. Two networks with the same number of weights can sit on opposite sides of the dartboard once you account for implicit regularisation from the optimiser. See [[./test-error-decomposition]] for the related approximation/estimation/optimization split.
</Callout>

<Callout title="Sample size">
All three terms depend on $n$ implicitly through $\bar f$ and $\mathrm{Var}_S[\hat f_S]$. As $n \to \infty$, variance falls; bias is the asymptote.
</Callout>
