This is the in-class version of the material developed in [[example-paper|the bias-variance refresher]]. The goal is for students to *feel* the trade-off, not just write it down. It fits in a single 50-minute session.

## Warm-up

We start with the picture, not the formula. Ask the room:

> "If I trained the same model on a hundred different training sets drawn from the same world, what would the hundred fits look like at a single test point?"

Two answers cover the space: *all clustered in the same spot, but the spot is wrong* (high bias, low variance) and *all over the place, but centred on the truth* (low bias, high variance). Draw both on the board before introducing any math.

## Exercise

Hand out the worksheet built from [[example-project|bv-sim]]. Each student gets a different random seed.

1. Fit polynomial regressors of degree $d \in \{1, 3, 7, 12\}$ to $n = 30$ samples from a noisy cubic.
2. Repeat 50 times with independent training sets.
3. At a fixed test point $x_0 = 0.7$, plot all 50 predictions for each $d$.
4. From those points, compute $\widehat{\text{bias}^2}$ and $\widehat{\text{variance}}$.

## Discussion prompts

- For which $d$ is bias largest? Why is the answer obvious before computing anything?
- For which $d$ is variance largest? Predict, then check.
- What happens to both as $n$ grows from 30 to 300?
- We assumed squared loss. What would change for 0-1 loss? (See [[machine-learning-theory]] for hints.)

## Closing

Send students out with the takeaway: *choosing a model class is choosing how to spend a fixed budget across bias, variance, and an irreducible noise floor*. The classroom exercise is the simplest setting in which that sentence becomes operational.
